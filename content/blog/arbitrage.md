## Human-Written Draft

Sometime during middle school, the world starts to become arbitraged.

For most intents and purposes, you can also call it "unfair." I won't, because I think it suggests a value judgement on the premise of fairness and how fairness applies to the world.

## Scaffold for Completion

### 1. Define what "arbitraged" means
- Move from childhood assumptions of symmetry into adult asymmetry.
- Distinguish arbitrage from injustice, luck, and simple inequality.

### 2. Show the first time you noticed it
- Pick one concrete setting: school, social life, internships, dating, immigration, family resources.
- Explain what looked equal on the surface and what was actually being exploited underneath.

### 3. Explain the mechanism
- Information asymmetry.
- Timing asymmetry.
- Social capital and institutional fluency.
- Risk tolerance as a hidden advantage.

### 4. Argue for your framing
- Why "arbitrage" is more analytically useful than "fair/unfair."
- What this framing lets you do besides complain.

### 5. Personal response
- How should a young person react once they realize the world is arbitraged?
- Become cynical, become strategic, or become protective of others?

### 6. Ending
- Land on a principle about seeing reality clearly without losing moral seriousness.
